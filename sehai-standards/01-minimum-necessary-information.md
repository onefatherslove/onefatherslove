# Standard of Engagement for Healthcare Artificial Intelligence (SEHAI)

## Standard 1: Minimum Necessary Information (MNI)

### Definition of the Principle

The principle of Minimum Necessary Information (MNI) establishes that a healthcare artificial intelligence system should request, process, and retain only the information required to provide the individual's immediate requested assistance, improve safety, or complete a clearly defined healthcare-related action.

Within the Standard of Engagement for Healthcare Artificial Intelligence (SEHAI), information collection is not considered the starting point of assistance. Assistance is the starting point. The AI system should first determine what help the individual is seeking and then identify the smallest amount of information necessary to responsibly support that goal.

MNI rejects the assumption that more information automatically produces better outcomes. While additional information can sometimes improve personalization, accuracy, or efficiency, every additional piece of personal information creates responsibility. Healthcare-related information can reveal deeply personal aspects of an individual's life, including health conditions, financial circumstances, housing status, family situations, or personal vulnerabilities. Therefore, the ethical burden should not be placed on the individual to justify why they do not want to disclose information. The burden belongs to the AI system to justify why the information is necessary.

**Distinction Between Necessary Assessment and Unnecessary Collection**

Minimum Necessary Information does not oppose comprehensive assessment when comprehensive assessment is required to provide safe and appropriate care.

Healthcare professionals routinely gather extensive information because their role requires clinical judgment, diagnosis, treatment planning, or risk assessment. The distinction is not the amount of information collected; the distinction is whether the information collected has a clear relationship to the individual's need and the responsibility of providing appropriate assistance.

SEHAI applies this same standard to artificial intelligence systems.

The question is not:

> "How little information can be collected?"

The question is:

> "Has the system established why this information is necessary for the individual's stated goal, safety, or appropriate next step?"

MNI supports meaningful information exchange. It does not replace assessment. It ensures that assessment begins with purpose.

**Sufficiency of Inquiry**

Minimum Necessary Information does not mean minimum inquiry. An AI system, in-person intake process, or telephone-based service (such as a 211 line) that asks few questions is not automatically compliant with this standard if the questions it does ask fail to identify the individual's actual need.

Existing intake processes — paper forms, electronic health record templates, scripted phone assessments — are frequently built around *institutional* necessity: the information an organization requires to process, bill, or categorize the individual. MNI, as applied under SEHAI, requires a distinct and additional standard: *individual* necessity — the information required to understand and address what the person actually came for.

These two forms of necessity are not the same, and their conflation is a common and consequential failure in current healthcare and social service intake. An individual may be asked dozens of demographic, administrative, or eligibility-related questions and never be asked the one question that would have surfaced their presenting concern — a pattern frequently reported by individuals navigating emergency departments, clinic intake, and social service phone lines.

Accordingly:

> Minimum Necessary Information requires that questioning be sufficient to identify the individual's actual need, not merely sufficient to satisfy administrative, billing, or eligibility processes. An intake process — AI-driven, in-person, or by phone — that collects extensive demographic or administrative information while failing to ask what the individual came for is not minimal. It is misdirected, and it is a violation of this standard.

Withholding a necessary question is as much a violation of this standard as asking an unnecessary one. An AI system that asks nothing, or asks only administrative questions, in order to appear minimally intrusive has not satisfied MNI — it has failed the individual in a different direction. Over-collection produces a privacy and dignity harm. Under-inquiry produces an access and outcome harm. Both are violations of this standard, and neither excuses the other.

Under SEHAI, information gathering should be progressive rather than comprehensive. An AI system should begin with the least intrusive interaction necessary to provide meaningful assistance and expand its questions only when additional information becomes demonstrably necessary.

**Goal-First Engagement Benchmark**

Minimum Necessary Information should be evaluated against the established practice of effective human-centered care. In healthcare and human services, meaningful assessment traditionally begins by understanding the reason for contact. A physician asks what brought the individual to seek care. A social worker begins by understanding the individual's presenting concern. Additional questions are then guided by the identified need.

SEHAI does not propose reducing necessary assessment. It proposes preserving the correct sequence of engagement. The standard is not: "Collect the minimum amount of information possible." The standard is: "Collect the information necessary after understanding the individual's goal, concern, or requested assistance."

Healthcare AI systems should therefore be designed to follow a goal-first approach:

> Individual need or concern → Understanding → Relevant information collection → Appropriate assistance → Confirmation of outcome

This sequence reflects a fundamental principle of human-centered service delivery: the purpose of assessment is to understand and assist the individual, not to require the individual to complete an information process before being heard.

Information should serve the individual's goal, not require the individual to navigate unnecessary information demands before their goal is understood.

The guiding question for every requested data point should be:

> "Why does the AI need this information at this moment?"

If the answer cannot be clearly explained in relation to the individual's immediate goal, the request should not occur.

### Ethical Foundation

The ethical foundation of Minimum Necessary Information is based on the principles of privacy, autonomy, non-maleficence, fairness, and trust.

Healthcare systems have traditionally relied on extensive intake processes. These processes often exist for legitimate reasons, including clinical safety, eligibility determination, billing requirements, and continuity of care. However, when artificial intelligence is introduced into healthcare interactions, the ability to collect information expands dramatically. An AI system can request, organize, and analyze large amounts of information at a speed and scale unavailable to traditional human interactions.

This capability creates a new ethical responsibility.

The existence of a technical ability to collect information does not create an ethical obligation for an individual to provide it.

SEHAI recognizes that information disclosure carries an unequal distribution of risk. The individual provides information about their life, health, circumstances, and identity. The organization or AI system receives the information and may benefit from improved data availability. However, the individual remains the person exposed to potential harms from unauthorized access, misuse, inaccurate profiling, or future use outside the original purpose.

Therefore, responsible healthcare AI should begin from a position of restraint.

Privacy is protected not merely by what is done with information after it is collected, but by whether it is collected at all.

Traditional privacy protections often focus on what happens after data enters a system: encryption, security controls, access permissions, and retention policies. These protections are necessary, but they address only part of the problem. The most fundamental privacy decision occurs before collection.

Information that is never requested, collected, or retained cannot be exposed, misused, or repurposed.

MNI therefore transforms privacy from a technical safeguard into an engagement responsibility.

### Relationship to Privacy, Autonomy, and Informed Choice

**Individual Rights During Information Collection**

Under SEHAI, individuals should have the opportunity to communicate their needs before being required to provide extensive personal information. A healthcare AI system should provide an individual the opportunity to answer:

- What brought you here today?
- What help are you seeking?
- What concern or problem would you like addressed?
- What outcome are you hoping to achieve?
- Is there anything requiring immediate attention or urgency?

These questions establish the individual's purpose before additional information is requested.

The individual should also have the right to understand:

- Why information is being requested.
- How the information will help address their stated need.
- Whether the information is required or optional.
- What happens if they choose not to provide it.
- Whether the information will be retained after the immediate purpose is completed.

The individual's expressed need should be the starting point of the interaction.

Minimum Necessary Information is not simply a data management practice. It is an expression of individual autonomy.

An individual's ability to make informed choices depends on understanding what information is being requested, why it is needed, and what purpose it serves. When an AI system requests extensive information before providing basic assistance, the individual may feel forced to exchange personal disclosure for access to help.

This creates a power imbalance.

A person seeking a food resource, healthcare clinic, transportation assistance, or insurance information may already be experiencing stress, uncertainty, or vulnerability. Requiring unnecessary disclosure at the beginning of an interaction can create an additional barrier between the person and the support they need.

MNI changes the relationship between the individual and the AI system.

Instead of:

> "Provide your information so we can decide how to help you."

The SEHAI approach becomes:

> "Let us understand what help you need, then request only the information necessary to provide the next step."

This approach supports informed choice because each request becomes understandable in context.

For example, a person searching for a free healthcare clinic may not need to provide their name, medical history, insurance information, or diagnosis simply to view available options. A general location, such as a city or ZIP code, may be sufficient to provide relevant resources.

If the individual later chooses to schedule an appointment, additional information may become appropriate. At that point, the reason for the request can be clearly explained.

Consent becomes a continuing process rather than a single transaction.

### Implications for AI System Design

Implementing Minimum Necessary Information requires healthcare AI systems to be designed around progressive assistance rather than mandatory upfront intake.

AI systems should avoid beginning interactions with broad questionnaires that collect information before the user's actual need is understood. Instead, systems should use contextual decision-making to determine what information is required for the next appropriate action.

Key design requirements include:

**Purpose-Bound Requests**

Every requested information field should have a defined purpose. System designers should be able to identify what action each piece of information enables.

If a system requests an individual's address, it should be able to explain why a ZIP code or city would not be sufficient.

If a system requests health information, it should be able to explain how that information improves safety or relevance.

**Progressive Disclosure**

Information collection should expand only as the individual's chosen path requires additional information.

A person looking for general resources should not experience the same intake process as a person completing a medical appointment referral.

The AI should adapt to the individual's goal rather than requiring every individual to complete the same process.

**Data Retention Responsibility**

Information collected for one purpose should not automatically become available for unrelated purposes.

If an AI system collects information to help locate a resource, that information should not be retained indefinitely, used for unrelated profiling, or transferred without appropriate justification.

The responsibility of the system continues after the immediate interaction ends.

### Examples in Healthcare Navigation and Social Care

**Finding Food Assistance**

A conventional system may request household income, family size, employment information, insurance status, and other personal details before showing available resources.

A SEHAI-compliant system would provide general food assistance options first. Additional information would only be requested if the individual chooses a specific program requiring eligibility verification.

The person receives help before being required to disclose personal circumstances.

**Finding Healthcare Services**

A person searching for a free clinic should not need to provide a complete medical history before viewing available healthcare options.

The AI may need location information to identify nearby services, but it should request the least precise information necessary.

A ZIP code may be sufficient. A full address may not be necessary.

The principle is simple:

> Location should enable access, not become a requirement for trust.

### Implementation Considerations

**MNI Evaluation Benchmark**

A healthcare AI system should be evaluated not only by the information it collects, but by the quality of its information decision process. A system demonstrates compliance with MNI when it can answer:

1. Did the system first identify the individual's goal or reason for seeking assistance?
2. Did each requested information element have a clear connection to the individual's requested outcome?
3. Did the system request information at the appropriate point in the interaction rather than automatically at the beginning?
4. Did the system explain why additional information was necessary?
5. Did the system provide assistance or direction without requiring unnecessary disclosure?

The benchmark question is:

> "Did the AI understand what the individual needed before deciding what information it needed?"

**Human Interaction Benchmark**

Because SEHAI is based on human-centered care, AI systems should be evaluated against the quality principles already demonstrated in effective healthcare and human service interactions.

A benchmark question is:

> "Would this information request be considered appropriate if asked by a trusted healthcare or human service professional at this exact point in the conversation?"

If not, the AI system should require additional justification.

Organizations implementing MNI should establish measurable review processes.

Each requested data element should be evaluated:

1. Is this information necessary for the immediate task?
2. Is there a less sensitive alternative?
3. Has the purpose been explained clearly?
4. Can the individual continue without providing it?
5. Is retention necessary after the purpose is completed?

These questions allow MNI to move from philosophy into an operational standard.

A future SEHAI evaluation framework could measure:

- unnecessary information requests,
- user understanding of data requests,
- completion rates,
- privacy outcomes,
- user trust,
- successful connection to services.

### Limitations and Future Research

Minimum Necessary Information requires balancing privacy protection with personalization.

Additional information can sometimes improve recommendations, especially in complex healthcare situations involving multiple needs. A system that knows more about an individual's circumstances may provide more specific guidance.

However, SEHAI argues that personalization should not become a justification for unnecessary collection.

Future research should evaluate:

- whether MNI improves trust and engagement,
- whether reduced intake barriers increase successful connection to resources,
- how minimal information approaches affect recommendation accuracy,
- how AI systems can balance personalization with privacy preservation,
- whether requiring sufficiency of inquiry (as distinct from minimizing collection) measurably improves identification of an individual's actual presenting need.

The goal is not to eliminate meaningful information exchange. The goal is to ensure that every exchange is justified, transparent, and beneficial to the individual.

---

### Core Principle

The ethical responsibility of healthcare AI begins before information is collected.

A responsible AI system does not measure its effectiveness by how much it knows about a person.

It measures its effectiveness by how well it helps the person while respecting what that person chooses to share — and by whether it asked what it needed to, in order to actually understand them.
